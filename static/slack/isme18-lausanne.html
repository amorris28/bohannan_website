Hey, everyone. Update on meals for ISME: In your post-trip expense report (which you will have access to once your pre-trip approval is completed), there is a per diem table that lists the amount of per diem you can receive for each meal. You can select which meals you would like to be reimbursed for on each day of business travel. For Lausanne, these are the amounts: ```$53 for breakfast $68 for lunch $90 for dinner $30 incidentals``` The way Brendan, Maria, the travel office, et al. would like us to handle this is to keep track of our food expenses each day and then in your Concur expense report just select the combination of meals that approximately covers how much you spent. So, for example, if you spent $50, you would decline everything but breakfast (since breakfast is $53). If you spent $80 you would decline lunch and dinner, but accept breakfast and incidentals since that sums to $53 + $30 = $83. etc. You do not need to keep meal receipts to attach to your Concur expense report. You just need to keep a record of your own expenses so you know how much to request in Concur. Brendan would like us to be frugal on meals, since there are no travel funds covering our per diem - it's just coming out of research funds. Let me know if you have any questions!

Looking forward to seeing you all at lab meeting today. In addition to seeing your beautiful posters, I?m looking forward to talking about how to get the most out of this scientific meeting. I think you get the most out of meetings if you understand the purpose of scientific meetings, if you identify your personal goals, and if you create a strategy for yourself before the meeting. I?ll outline each of these ideas briefly below (and we can talk more about them at lab meeting).

bohannan
2022-08-05 14:45
Why are there scientific meetings? There are at least three primary reasons for big scientific meetings: 1. to efficiently transfer information (esp new discoveries, work in progress and unpublished research), 2. to build community (which some call ?networking?), and 3. to socially construct a discipline (to reinforce disciplinary values, culture and traditions). Ask me at our meeting and I can ?unpack? each of these reasons.

bohannan
2022-08-05 14:46
To me, a successful scientific meeting starts with identifying one?s personal goals for a meeting. There are many goals one may have?to learn something new, to meet interesting/inspiring/like-minded/creative people, to get help with your research, to meet prospective employers, to draw attention to your ideas or discoveries, to have fun, to feel re-inspired about science, etc. etc. What are your goals?

bohannan
2022-08-05 14:46
Once you have id?d your goals, you can develop a strategy. For example, if you have as a goal to draw attention to your ideas, you might want to invite specific people to your poster or talk. If you have as a goal to meet prospective employers, you might want to look over a list of vendors at the meeting or find out if there is a physical or virtual ?job board? at the meeting. If a goal is to meet specific people, you might want to find out if someone in our group already knows them and ask for an introduction. If your goal is to learn as much as you can, you might want to look over the abstracts and titles of talks beforehand, and develop a triaged schedule for talks and posters you want to see.

bohannan
2022-08-05 14:47
Having a well-defined strategy ahead of time helps you avoid a very common mistake made at meetings ? succumbing to FOMO. This has many versions. For example, FOMO can cause you to attend too many talks, reducing your opportunities for reflection and for meeting other people. Or FOMO can result in you staying up late every night to talk science and socialize, and then burning out before you get to the end of the meeting. Having a plan helps you avoid this.

Hey, everyone. I ran into some issues getting my poster PDF ready and so wanted to share how I solved it in case anyone runs into similar issues. Hopefully everyone else's goes smoothly and you don't need to do any of this! 1. The print office wants your PDF in PDF/X-3 format. If you don't have Adobe Acrobat on your computer, you can walk over to the Design library on the same floor as the print office and use the library computers to convert your PDF to the correct format using the poster printing instructions. 2. I found that when I converted my PDF to PDF/X-3 the resolution of my figures went way down. I had exported them from R as PNG images with 300 DPI. I solved this by instead saving them as SVG, which makes them vector graphics so they won't be pixelated. You should be able to import SVG images into Powerpoint the same way you use JPEGs or PNGs. Then, when you convert your PDF to X-3 they should be high resolution. 3. Another thing to check is when you export your PowerPoint slide to a PDF, it might be compressing your images. By default, mine had "Reduce Image Resolution to 300 DPI" and "JPEG Compression 90%". I had to turn both of those off and set the Images to "Lossless Compression" to get full-resolution images. Here is the command to export your ggplot to an SVG: ```ggsave("plot.svg", plot, height = whatever, width = whatever)```
