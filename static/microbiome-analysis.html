2021-05-14 03:39
https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2021.05.10.443486v1.full.pdf Pretty interesting pre-print of a comparison of differential abundance test tools. They test pretty much all DA tools I've heard of including ones that require rarefied data and ones that don't. And they test them across 38 datasets (from various environments). They find that tools that require rarefying and ones that don't both perform pretty much equally well in terms of identifying the same significant ASVs across tools and similar false-discovery rates. They also point out that whether or not you rarefy has a pretty minor effect compared to other data decisions (this is just for differential abundance, not for diversity metrics). They recommend filtering out ASVs that are only present in 10% of samples or fewer, because these low-prevalence taxa seriously hamper multiple testing corrections and thus lower the sensitivity of the tests. Also, variation in sequencing depth is correlated with the number of significant ASVs in pretty much every tool. Overall, this paper concluded that ALDEx2 and ANCOM-II performed the best and were the most conservative (they had low false-discovery rates and identified a smaller number of taxa, but those taxa were identified by many other tools) and thus are also less sensitive. Another similar study identified different tools as the best: DESeq2 and corncob. So, I think any of those are valid choices. They also recommend running multiple of these tools in your analysis and then comparing the consensus taxa. I usually use Corncob on non-rarefied data without filtering low-prevalence taxa. But I think in the future I will try ANCOM-II and start filtering taxa that are present in <10% of samples.

As we talked about in lab meeting, it seems like the compositional data analysis (CoDA) methods are growing in popularity and are becoming the new standard. That being said, depending on your data, it may be that the "simpler" traditional methods, such as converting your counts to proportions and calculating Bray-Curtis dissimilarity, can provide similar conclusions. Though, your mileage may vary with what reviewers ask you to do. If you want to start using compositional methods, a good place to start is the Aitchison dissimilarity metric, which is Euclidean distance after center-log ratio transformation (a.k.a, clr). The clr transformation takes the read count for a particular OTU in a particular sample and divides it by the geometric mean of all of the OTUs in that sample and log transforms that ratio. In this way, you can control for differences in library counts between samples while preserving the ratios between individual taxa. Do not perform any transformations on the read counts before clr - you want to use the raw reads. To do this in R, I recommend using the `microViz` package function `dist_calc` or `tax_transform`. For example, if you want to plot an ordination you would do this: ```library(tidyverse) library(phyloseq) library(microViz) phyloseq %>% tax_transform(trans = "clr") %>% ord_calc(method = "PCA") %>% ord_plot()``` If you want to calculate Aitchison dissimilarities and use them in another analysis, such as a PERMANOVA, you would do this: ```phyloseq %>% dist_calc(dist = "aitchison") %>% dist_permanova()``` You don't necessarily have to use the `microViz` functions. You could just pull out the Aitchison dissimilarities and use them in `adonis2` or manually create an ordination with `ggplot2` or whatever you're familiar with. The `transform` function from the `microbiome` package performs the actual clr transformation under the hood. If you're having trouble with any of this, feel free to ask me in Slack. As a side note, if you want to use CoDa methods for differential abundance analysis, one option is `ALDEx2` . I haven't figured it out yet, but I intend to try it in future analyses. Some good references: Here is the original Aitchison citation: Aitchison, J. (1982). The Statistical Analysis of Compositional Data. _Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B (Methodological)_, _44_(2), 139?160. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2517-6161.1982.tb01195.x This is a good review of compositional data analysis for microbiome data: Calle, M. L. (2019). Statistical Analysis of Metagenomics Data. _Genomics & Informatics_, _17_(1), e6. https://doi.org/10.5808/GI.2019.17.1.e6 Here is an extensive blog post about microbiome analysis using CoDA methods in R. It is currently my go-to reference for these types of analyses: https://www.nicholas-ollberding.com/post/introduction-to-the-statistical-analysis-of-microbiome-data-in-r/
